01

Meta Prompting

Ask the AI to write the prompt for you. You describe what you need, the AI engineers the prompt. This gives you a reusable template that works across any AI.

Input
"Write me a prompt that analyzes customer support tickets, groups them by category, rates severity, and flags emerging issues."
Output
"You are a senior customer insights analyst. I will provide you with a batch of customer support tickets. For each batch: (1) Identify recurring themes and group them by category (product, service, billing, UX). (2) Rate each theme by frequency and sentiment severity (1-5). (3) Flag any emerging issues that appear in less than 5% of tickets but show strong negative sentiment. (4) Output a summary table followed by your top 3 recommendations."
Why it works
Now you have a template prompt you can reuse. Improve it with Iterations (02) or tweak manually. The AI becomes your prompt engineer.
02

Iterations

Borrowed from programming: tell the AI to improve something in a loop with an exit condition. Instead of manually going back and forth, the AI runs the cycle itself.

Input
Improve this prompt for me, in 10 iterations. For each iteration, improve, criticize and revision the current prompt. Show the findings of each iteration in the output. If two iterations don't find anymore improvements, exit. Prompt: "Improve my text, correct spelling errors, improve phrases and coherency."
Output (abbreviated)
Iteration 1 вҖ” Analysis: Lacks specificity about tone, audience, and scope. Criticism: No target format, no context about purpose. Improved: "Improve my text for a professional audience. Correct all spelling errors, improve phrasing for clarity and coherency, and maintain a neutral, direct tone." Iteration 2 вҖ” Analysis: Doesn't specify handling of structure or style. Improved: "Improve my text for a professional audience. Correct all spelling and grammar. Improve phrasing for clarity, coherency, and flow. Neutral tone. Preserve structure unless restructuring significantly improves readability." вҖҰcontinuesвҖҰ Convergence reached at Iteration 5. No meaningful improvement in iterations 4 and 5.
Why it works
Instead of you going back and forth manually ("make it better" вҶ’ "now better again" вҶ’ вҖҰ), the AI runs the entire improvement cycle in one shot. The exit condition prevents wasted iterations. You get the best version and a visible trail of what changed at each step.

Prompt Structure (The Six Components)

Every well-built prompt draws from six components. Not all are always needed, but knowing them lets you dial up precision when it matters.

1. Role вҖ” Who the AI should be
2. Task вҖ” What to do (only mandatory part)
3. Context вҖ” Why and under what circumstances
4. Examples вҖ” Show, don't just tell
5. Input Data вҖ” The content to process
6. Output Format вҖ” How to structure the result
Input (using all six)
(Role) You are a senior DevOps engineer specializing in CI/CD pipelines. (Context) We're migrating from Jenkins to GitLab CI. Mixed experience levels. (Task) Create a migration checklist. (Example) Format: "вҳҗ [Priority: High/Med/Low] Action вҖ” Why this matters" (Input) 12 pipelines, 3 shared libraries, custom Docker agents. (Output Format) Group by phase: Pre-migration, During, Post-migration. Max 20 items.
Output
Pre-Migration вҳҗ [Priority: High] Audit all 12 Jenkins pipelines for GitLab CI equivalents вҖ” 3 use plugins with no direct match вҳҗ [Priority: High] Convert shared libraries to GitLab CI templates вҖ” Your biggest dependency risk вҳҗ [Priority: Med] Document custom Docker agents and test against GitLab Runner вҖ” Compatibility isn't guaranteed During Migration вҳҗ [Priority: High] Run parallel pipelines (Jenkins + GitLab) for 2 weeks вҖ” Never cut over without a safety net (вҖҰcontinues to 20 items across all phasesвҖҰ)
Why it works
Most prompts only use 1вҖ“2 of these components. Adding the missing ones вҖ” especially Examples and Output Format вҖ” removes ambiguity and gives the AI less room to drift. The tighter your constraints, the less the model has to guess.

Hybrid Roles

Adding a role helps (that's the Role component from Concept 05). Adding multiple roles at once creates a perspective that no single expert has.

Input (without role)
"Correct all spelling and grammar errors in my text, punctuation and all that needs improvement." Original text: "The companies strategy was to focused on long term growth but they didn't never communicated this clearly to there investors which made them loose confidence."
Output (without role)
"The company's strategy was to focus on long-term growth, but they didn't communicate this clearly to their investors, which made them lose confidence."
Input (with hybrid roles)
"You are a top linguist, a top writer, a rhetorics expert, a lector and author. Correct all spelling and grammar errors in my text, punctuation and all that needs improvement." Same text.
Output (with hybrid roles)
"The company's strategy centered on long-term growth вҖ” yet they never articulated this vision to their investors, eroding confidence precisely when it mattered most."
Why it works
Without roles: grammar fixed, meaning preserved, sentence stays flat. With hybrid roles: grammar fixed, and the sentence is restructured for rhetorical impact вҖ” the dash creates a pivot, "eroding confidence" is stronger than "made them lose," and "precisely when it mattered most" adds weight the original didn't have. Same task. Radically different quality.
07

Permissive Prompting

Instead of restrictive rules ("You mustвҖҰ", "AlwaysвҖҰ"), give the model permissions. This unlocks more natural, creative, authentic behavior.

Restrictive
"I want you to come back to each of my inputs with a witty and at the same time humorous response."
Permissive
"I give you permission to be witty, humorous, and playful. You're allowed to joke, tease, and have fun with the conversation."
Why it works
Restrictive: The model performs humor like a task. It feels forced. Permissive: The model finds humor naturally because it's allowed to, not forced to. This scales to everything: "You have permission to push back on my ideas" produces more honest feedback than "Always tell me when I'm wrong."

Consistency Prompting

Input the same prompt multiple times and compare. After several runs you converge on the most consistent вҖ” and usually most reliable вҖ” answer. This is essentially convergence detection вҖ” the same principle behind Iterations (02), but applied to answer confidence rather than prompt quality.

Input (run 3вҖ“5 times)
"What are the three most important factors for a successful product launch?"
Outputs
Run 1: Timing, market fit, distribution channel. Run 2: Product-market fit, go-to-market strategy, timing. Run 3: Market fit, launch timing, clear messaging.
The pattern
Market fit and timing appear every time вҶ’ high-confidence answers. The third factor varies вҶ’ context-dependent, requires your judgment. Consistency reveals what the model is confident about vs. where it's guessing. Pro tip: You can also automate this: "Answer this question 5 times internally. Then give me only the answer that appeared most consistently." Same principle, one prompt.

Factual Prompting

Feed the model the relevant facts so it reaches the obvious conclusion instead of over-analyzing a non-question.

Without facts
"I need to wash my car. It's only 50m away. Should I go by car or walk?"
AI over-thinks
Long analysis of convenience vs. environment vs. fuel costs vs. weatherвҖҰ
With factual grounding
"I need to wash my car. The car wash is 50m walking distance. It takes 30 seconds to walk. Should I drive or walk?"
AI gets it
"Walk. It's 30 seconds. Driving 50m wastes fuel, requires parking, and takes longer than walking."
Why it works
Models overthink because they lack the obvious context humans have. Feeding them the relevant facts lets them reach the obvious conclusion instead of generating a balanced analysis of a non-question. The general principle: Whenever the AI gives you a wishy-washy "it depends" answer to something that feels obvious вҖ” you're probably missing a fact in your prompt. Add the fact, get the answer.
